I appreciate you pushing for more substance. To move past the high-level "vibe" and into the tactical mechanics that Aaron Epstein, Garry Tan, and guests (like the founders of Linear and Framer) actually use during their roasts, we need to look at the site as a series of functional modules.
Based on the most recent 2025 and 2026 YC Design Reviews, here is the comprehensive guide to building a "YC-standard" website.
1. The "First Impression" Audit (The 5-Second Rule)
YC partners often start by covering the top half of the screen and asking: "If I can't explain this to my mom in one sentence, you've lost."
 * The "H1" Clear-Text Mandate:
   * The Error: Using "The AI-driven synergy platform for tomorrow."
   * The Fix: Use a Verb-Noun-Benefit structure. "Automate [Task] using [Method] to save [Time/Money]."
 * The Sub-headline Support: This should strictly define the target audience. "Built for DevOps teams managing 100+ microservices."
 * The Hero Image Logic: * If it's a SaaS/Tool: Show the most "impressive" dashboard or a code snippet.
   * If it's Infrastructure: Show a simplified diagram of how it sits between the user and their data.
2. Information Architecture: The "Modular Trust" Flow
YC critiques typically follow a specific flow of information. If you break this order, you create cognitive friction.
| Section | Purpose | YC "Roast" Feedback |
|---|---|---|
| Header | Navigation | "Hide your 'About Us' and 'Careers' in the footer. Keep the header focused on Product, Pricing, and Login." |
| Social Proof | Credibility | "Don't just show logos. Show a quote from a 'Relevant Peer' (e.g., a CTO if you're selling to CTOs)." |
| The "How it Works" | De-risking | "Use 3 clear steps. If it takes more than 3 steps to explain, your product is too complex." |
| Feature Bento | Depth | "Stop using generic icons. Every feature needs a mini-screenshot of the actual UI." |
| The Anti-Objection | Comparison | "Include a 'How we are different from [Competitor]' section. Founders are often too scared to name names." |
3. The "AI Slop" & UX Anti-Patterns
In recent 2026 episodes, "AI Slop" is the #1 target of criticism.
 * Ghost Loaders & Fade-ins: Aaron Epstein often critiques sites where text "fades in" as you scroll. The Verdict: It's annoying and slows down the user's ability to scan for info.
 * The "Sparkle" Overuse: Using the "✨" emoji to signal AI is now considered lazy.
   * The Alternative: Show the Result of the AI (e.g., a finished report, a fixed bug, a generated image) rather than the "magic" of it.
 * Scroll-Jacking: Never take control of the user's scroll speed to show an animation. YC partners advocate for "Linear Scrolling"—the user should always feel in control.
4. Technical Design (The Developer Standard)
If you're building for a technical audience (the core of the 2026 RFS), you must follow the "Linear/Stripe Standard":
 * Typography: Use a high-legibility sans-serif (Inter, Geist, or SF Pro).
 * Dark Mode by Default: For developer tools, dark mode isn't a feature; it's the expectation.
 * Keyboard Shortcuts: Explicitly show Cmd + K or / shortcuts in the UI. It signals that you’ve built a "Pro" tool, not a "Toy."
 * Latency as Marketing: If your AI agent is fast, put the latency speed (e.g., "Sub-200ms inference") right in the hero section.
5. The "Conversion" Checklist
Finally, the "Call to Action" (CTA) must be aggressive and clear.
 * The Primary CTA: Should be a high-contrast color (often the "YC Orange" or a bold brand color).
 * The "No-Risk" Signal: Place "No credit card required" or "Set up in 2 minutes" directly under the button.
 * Pricing Transparency: YC is famous for hating "Contact Sales" for early-stage startups. Show a "Free" or "Starter" tier to get users into the funnel immediately.
